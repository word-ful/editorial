I almost ignored the connection request. No mutuals, default template, the kind I delete forty times a week.

But this one had a three-sentence note that referenced a piece I'd written about scope creep and quoted the one line I'd agonized over. It asked for nothing: no calendar link, no "quick 15 minutes to explore synergies." I accepted.

Six months later, that connection sent a referral that turned into real work. That interaction is now the exception, and the reason has nothing to do with etiquette.

LinkedIn functions as a CRM, and we've all been pretending otherwise. An entire venture-backed category (LeadDelta, Folk, Breakcold, Octopus) is built on the thesis that LinkedIn is, functionally, a sales database. The pitch slap is the product working as designed, and "just personalize more" doesn't fix incentive design.

So if it's a CRM, treat it like one. The note that landed referenced a specific artifact rather than generic praise, offered something before asking for anything, and was open about being outreach instead of dressing the ask up as friendship.

Most competitors are paying for software that generates the opposite at scale, which means writing one real sentence to one real person is now differentiating. The platform won't reward you for it and the algorithm won't surface it. But the connections that turn into real work — the referrals, the late-night calls about a fundraise — come from people who reached out anyway, like the rest of the internet wasn't broken.

Be that person, or hit accept when you find one. What made the last great connection request you accepted actually land?
