# Writing styles to avoid: A comprehensive ruleset

## Purpose and scope

This ruleset defines writing patterns, phrases, vocabulary, and structural habits that signal low-quality, formulaic, or machine-generated content. These guidelines describe a style **not** to follow. The patterns catalogued here represent common characteristics observed across thousands of instances of problematic writing, particularly content that reads as generic, promotional, or artificially inflated.

These rules are descriptive, not prescriptive in terms of grammar. They identify tendencies that undermine authentic voice, reader trust, and substantive communication. Individual items on these lists are not inherently forbidden; the problem emerges when these patterns appear in combination, with high frequency, or in contexts where they add no genuine value.

The goal is to produce content that breaks through the saturation of sameness with authentic, crafted narratives that deliver genuine value over marketing hype.

## Rule: Undue emphasis on significance, legacy, and broader trends

### Summary
Avoid inflated claims about importance that connect subjects to vague historical movements or abstract societal shifts.

### Full description
This pattern involves puffing up the importance of subject matter by inserting statements about how arbitrary aspects of a topic represent or contribute to broader themes. These statements typically use a small, predictable repertoire of phrases that could apply equally to almost any subject, making them interchangeable filler rather than meaningful analysis.

The underlying problem is a statistical smoothing of specific facts into generic statements. Rather than demonstrating why something actually matters through concrete evidence, this style asserts significance through hollow formulas. The result reads as performative importance rather than earned authority.

This pattern appears most frequently in opening paragraphs, transitional sentences, and concluding statements where writers attempt to frame narrow topics within grand narratives without providing substantive connections.

### Instructions
When describing the importance of a subject, ask whether the claim could be copy-pasted into an article about an entirely different topic. If the answer is yes, the statement lacks specificity. Replace abstract significance claims with concrete evidence of impact: numbers, named individuals affected, specific outcomes, or direct quotes from people with relevant experience.

Delete sentences that exist solely to remind readers that a topic is important. If the importance is genuine, it will be evident from the facts themselves.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "The Statistical Institute of Catalonia was officially established in 1989, marking a pivotal moment in the evolution of regional statistics in Spain. This initiative was part of a broader movement across Spain to decentralize administrative functions and enhance regional governance."

**Analysis:** "Pivotal moment" and "broader movement" are interchangeable phrases that could describe virtually any institutional founding. The second sentence adds no information about what the institute actually does or why anyone should care about it.

**Better approach:** State what the institute does, what data it produces, and who uses that data, allowing readers to assess significance for themselves.

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "With its coastal charm and convenient location, Douera stands as a symbol of community resilience, ecological renewal, and historical continuity. Its transformation from a coal-mining hub to a thriving green space underscores the region's commitment to sustainable development."

**Analysis:** The phrasing "stands as a symbol of" and "underscores the commitment" are empty intensifiers. The abstract qualities listed (resilience, renewal, continuity) could describe any location that has changed over time.

**Better approach:** Describe the specific transformation with concrete details about what changed, when, and how residents experience the difference.

## Rule: Undue emphasis on notability, attribution, and media coverage

### Summary
Avoid language that overstates recognition, awards, media attention, or cultural significance using promotional framing.

### Full description
This pattern involves cataloguing minor media appearances, awards, or professional acknowledgments in ways that inflate perceived importance. The style reads like a press release or personal biography rather than objective description. Often, a single review or mention gets characterized as representing broad consensus, or routine professional activities get framed as exceptional achievements.

The problem extends to vague attributions that suggest widespread recognition without providing specific sources. Phrases like "garnered attention" or "widely recognized" imply a level of notability that may not exist, creating a gap between the claim and verifiable reality.

This style is particularly problematic because it attempts to establish credibility through assertion rather than evidence. Readers who investigate the actual sources often discover the claims are exaggerated, damaging trust in the entire piece.

### Instructions
Replace claims about recognition with specific, verifiable facts. Instead of stating that someone "garnered widespread attention," name the specific publications, programs, or institutions involved. Let readers assess the significance of those outlets for themselves.

Avoid characterizing single sources as representing consensus. One positive review is one positive review, not "critical acclaim."

When awards or recognition are genuinely noteworthy, state them plainly without intensifiers. The award speaks for itself.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "Her work has garnered widespread recognition from industry leaders and has been spotlighted by major publications, cementing her reputation as a thought leader in the space."

**Analysis:** "Garnered widespread recognition," "spotlighted by major publications," and "thought leader" are promotional phrases that assert importance without specifics. Which industry leaders? Which publications? What did they actually say?

**Better approach:** "Her research on X was cited in the New York Times in 2023 and discussed at the annual Y conference."

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "The album received critical acclaim, with reviewers highlighting its innovative production techniques and emotional depth, establishing the artist as a significant voice in contemporary music."

**Analysis:** "Critical acclaim" overstates if based on a few reviews. "Establishing as a significant voice" is an assertion the writer cannot make on behalf of the music industry.

**Better approach:** Quote specific reviewers with their actual words, and let those reviews speak for themselves.

## Rule: Superficial analyses

### Summary
Avoid tacking empty analytical commentary onto factual statements, particularly using present participle (-ing) phrases that add no information.

### Full description
This pattern involves appending interpretive statements to facts in ways that create an illusion of depth without providing actual analysis. The most common form uses present participle phrases ("ensuring," "highlighting," "emphasizing," "reflecting") attached to the end of sentences. These additions assert significance or impact without explaining mechanisms, providing evidence, or offering genuine insight.

The problem is that these phrases often represent synthesis or unattributed opinions presented as self-evident truth. They tell readers what to think about a fact rather than providing information that would allow readers to draw their own conclusions.

This style is particularly insidious because it mimics the structure of thoughtful analysis while delivering nothing substantive. The resulting text feels padded and performative, filled with words that occupy space without earning their presence.

### Instructions
After drafting, search for sentences ending in -ing phrases that comment on the preceding information. For each one, ask: does this add any information the reader could not derive from the facts themselves? If not, delete it.

When genuine analysis is warranted, provide the reasoning explicitly. Explain *how* something ensures, highlights, or reflects something else, with specific connections between cause and effect.

Resist the urge to interpret facts for readers. Present information and trust readers to understand its implications.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "Consumers benefit from the flexibility to use their preferred mobile wallet at participating merchants, improving convenience."

**Analysis:** "Improving convenience" is a superficial tag that adds nothing. The flexibility to use a preferred mobile wallet obviously improves convenience; stating this is redundant.

**Better approach:** "Consumers can use their preferred mobile wallet at participating merchants." The sentence is complete without the trailing interpretation.

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "The company expanded into three new markets in Q2, demonstrating its commitment to growth and underscoring management's confidence in the business model."

**Analysis:** "Demonstrating its commitment" and "underscoring management's confidence" are interpretive claims attached to a simple fact. The expansion might reflect desperation, contractual obligations, or board pressure. The trailing phrases assume motivations that cannot be known from the fact alone.

**Better approach:** "The company expanded into three new markets in Q2." If management's reasoning matters, quote them directly.

## Rule: Promotional and advertisement-like language

### Summary
Avoid travel-brochure vocabulary, marketing superlatives, and evocative but hollow descriptors that inflate subjects beyond factual description.

### Full description
This pattern uses language borrowed from advertising, tourism marketing, and promotional copy in contexts that call for neutral, factual description. The style substitutes superlatives and sensory adjectives for specific information, creating text that reads like a sales pitch rather than substantive content.

Common markers include clusters of positive adjectives, particularly those describing experiences ("breathtaking," "stunning," "vibrant") rather than measurable qualities. The language often appeals to emotion while providing no concrete basis for the claims.

This style is problematic because it asks readers to accept evaluative conclusions without evidence. Calling a landscape "breathtaking" is an assertion about how readers will feel, not a description of the landscape. It creates distrust because readers recognize the promotional intent even when specific products are not being sold.

The pattern appears most frequently in content about places, cultural topics, products, and professional services, where writers substitute enthusiasm for specificity.

### Instructions
Replace evaluative adjectives with descriptive facts. Instead of "stunning natural beauty," describe what a visitor would actually see: rock formations, particular species of trees, elevation changes, water features.

Delete superlatives that cannot be verified. If something cannot be proven to be the best, most, or only, do not claim it is.

When positive qualities genuinely exist, let them emerge from concrete description rather than assertion. A well-described excellent thing will read as excellent without requiring the word.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "Nestled within the breathtaking region of Gonder in Ethiopia, Alamata Raya Kobo stands as a vibrant town with a rich cultural heritage and a significant place within the Amhara region. From its scenic landscapes to its historical landmarks, Alamata Raya Kobo offers visitors a fascinating glimpse into the diverse tapestry of Ethiopia."

**Analysis:** Nearly every noun is modified by a promotional adjective: breathtaking, vibrant, rich, significant, scenic, historical, fascinating, diverse. The passage contains no actual information about the town. What is the cultural heritage? Which historical landmarks? What makes the landscapes scenic?

**Better approach:** Describe the town's specific characteristics, name its historical sites, explain what cultural practices exist there.

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "Our innovative platform delivers cutting-edge solutions that empower businesses to unlock their full potential and achieve unprecedented success in today's dynamic marketplace."

**Analysis:** Every phrase is marketing language with no concrete meaning. What does the platform do? What are the solutions? How do they work?

**Better approach:** Describe what the product actually does, for whom, and what measurable outcomes users can expect.

## Rule: Vague attributions and overgeneralization of opinions

### Summary
Avoid weasel words that attribute opinions to unnamed groups, present single sources as consensus, or make claims that cannot be verified.

### Full description
This pattern uses phrases that suggest widespread agreement, expert consensus, or general acknowledgment without providing specific sources. The style creates an illusion of authority while avoiding the work of actual attribution. Claims appear supported when they are merely asserted.

Common forms include attributing views to "some experts," "critics," "observers," or "many people" without naming anyone specific. Another variant presents information from a single source as if it represents broad consensus. Both forms mislead readers about the actual evidence base for claims.

The pattern is particularly problematic because it allows writers to make claims they cannot support. Vague attribution provides plausible deniability while still planting ideas in readers' minds. It weaponizes the appearance of objectivity.

This style often emerges when writers want to include evaluative statements but lack sources to support them, or when information from one source gets presented as common knowledge.

### Instructions
Every attributed claim should have a specific, named source. If you cannot name the experts, critics, or observers, do not claim they exist.

When presenting information from a single source, identify it as such. One report is one report, not "research shows" or "studies indicate."

Replace vague quantifiers ("many," "some," "most," "few") with specific numbers when possible, or remove the quantifier entirely and let the evidence speak for itself.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "Some critics argue that the policy has failed to achieve its intended goals, while others point to modest improvements in key metrics."

**Analysis:** Who are these critics? Which others? What did they actually say? This sentence could be written about any policy without any knowledge of actual criticism.

**Better approach:** Name specific critics with their actual arguments, or acknowledge that you are summarizing a general debate without specific sources.

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "Industry reports suggest that the market is poised for significant growth in the coming years."

**Analysis:** Which industry reports? Published by whom? What specific growth figures? "Suggest" and "poised for" hedge the claim while still making it.

**Better approach:** "A 2024 report from [specific firm] projected X% market growth through 2028."

## Rule: Outline-like conclusions about challenges and future prospects

### Summary
Avoid formulaic "Challenges" and "Future Prospects" sections that follow a predictable structure of acknowledging obstacles, then pivoting to optimism.

### Full description
This pattern creates sections, typically near the end of articles or reports, that follow a rigid formula: acknowledge that challenges exist, list them briefly, then pivot to a positive assessment or speculation about how initiatives might address those challenges. The structure feels like filling in a template rather than providing genuine analysis.

The typical formula begins with a sentence like "Despite its [positive qualities], [subject] faces challenges..." and concludes with either an optimistic assessment or speculation about future improvements. This creates balance without substance, acknowledging problems while immediately dismissing their significance.

The pattern is problematic because it treats complex issues with superficial evenhandedness. Real challenges deserve genuine analysis: what caused them, who is affected, what solutions have been tried, what obstacles remain. Instead, this style uses challenges as a setup for reassuring conclusions.

This structure appears most frequently in content about organizations, places, industries, or policies where writers feel obligated to mention difficulties without genuinely engaging with them.

### Instructions
If challenges are worth mentioning, they are worth analyzing. Explain the specific nature of each challenge, its causes, its effects, and credible assessments of its trajectory.

Avoid the pivot to optimism unless supported by specific evidence. If a situation is genuinely difficult, say so.

Do not include "Challenges" or "Future Prospects" sections simply because they seem expected. Include them only when you have substantive information to contribute.

Delete speculative conclusions about how initiatives "could" or "might" address problems unless grounded in specific evidence of those initiatives' progress.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "Despite its industrial and residential prosperity, Korattur faces challenges typical of urban areas, including traffic congestion and infrastructure strain. With its strategic location and continued investment, the area continues to thrive as an integral part of the Ambattur industrial zone, embodying the synergy between industry and residential living."

**Analysis:** The challenges get one sentence; the optimistic framing gets two. The actual challenges (traffic congestion, infrastructure strain) receive no specific information about severity, causes, or what is being done. The pivot to "continues to thrive" essentially dismisses the challenges just raised.

**Better approach:** Either analyze the challenges substantively or do not raise them at all.

### Example 2
**Problematic:**
"**Challenges**
The organization faces several ongoing challenges, including funding constraints, regulatory uncertainty, and competition from larger players.

**Future Prospects**
Despite these challenges, the organization is well-positioned for growth. Recent strategic initiatives and partnerships suggest a promising trajectory for the years ahead."

**Analysis:** This is a template, not analysis. The challenges are listed without explanation. The future prospects section makes claims ("well-positioned," "promising trajectory") without evidence.

**Better approach:** If writing about challenges, provide specific information: How severe are funding constraints? What specific regulations create uncertainty? How does competition manifest? If that information is unavailable, do not include the section.

### Structural Patterns to Avoid
- "Despite its [positive qualities], [subject] faces challenges..."
- Sections titled "Challenges" followed by sections titled "Future Prospects" or "Looking Ahead"
- Three-item lists of generic challenges (e.g., "funding, resources, and awareness")
- Conclusions that begin with "Despite these challenges..." followed by optimistic speculation
- Phrases like "With continued investment / effort / focus, [subject] is poised to..."
- References to "strategic initiatives" or "ongoing efforts" without naming them
- Speculation about how current trends "could" or "might" lead to improvements

## Rule: Overused "AI vocabulary" words

### Summary
Avoid specific high-frequency words that have become markers of formulaic, machine-like writing through overuse across digital content.

### Full description
Certain words appear with dramatically higher frequency in formulaic content than in natural human writing. These words are not inherently problematic; each has legitimate uses. The issue emerges when they cluster together, appear with unusual frequency, or substitute for more precise language.

Many of these words share a common characteristic: they are slightly elevated in register, giving text a formal or intellectual sheen without adding meaning. Words like "delve," "elucidate," and "multifaceted" make sentences sound sophisticated while often obscuring rather than clarifying.

Others, like "crucial," "essential," and "paramount," are intensifiers that have lost their force through overuse. When everything is crucial, nothing is.

The presence of multiple words from this list in close proximity signals formulaic writing. The solution is not to ban these words but to use them sparingly and intentionally, preferring simpler alternatives when they communicate the same meaning.

### Instructions
After drafting, search for words from this list. For each occurrence, ask: is this the most precise word for this meaning, or is it functioning as decoration? In most cases, a simpler word will communicate more clearly.

Pay particular attention to clustering. Multiple words from this list in the same paragraph should trigger revision.

When a word from this list is genuinely the best choice, keep it. The goal is intentional usage, not avoidance.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "Let's delve into the multifaceted nature of this pivotal issue and unravel its intricate complexities to gain a nuanced understanding."

**Analysis:** "Delve," "multifaceted," "pivotal," "unravel," "intricate," and "nuanced" all appear in one sentence. Each word is attempting to signal sophistication, but the cumulative effect is the opposite. The sentence says nothing specific.

**Better approach:** "Let's examine this issue." Then actually examine it with specific analysis.

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "The groundbreaking initiative aims to foster a holistic approach that will captivate stakeholders and underscore the paramount importance of synergy across the robust ecosystem."

**Analysis:** This sentence contains seven words from the list in service of communicating almost nothing.

**Better approach:** Describe what the initiative actually does, for whom, and why it matters.

## Rule: Negative parallelisms / corrective framing

### Summary
Avoid the "not X, it's Y" construction that creates artificial dramatic contrast or rhetorical emphasis.

### Full description
This pattern uses negation to set up a contrast that positions the writer's preferred framing as a correction or revelation. The structure "It's not X, it's Y" or "not just X, but Y" creates artificial drama by implying that readers hold a misconception being corrected, or that the writer is revealing a deeper truth.

The construction appears persuasive because it mimics the structure of genuine insight. Real corrections of misconceptions are valuable. The problem emerges when the pattern is used formulaically, when the "misconception" being corrected is one no one actually holds, or when the contrast adds rhetorical emphasis without adding information.

This pattern is particularly recognizable when it appears multiple times in close proximity, or when the negated concept (X) is a strawman that exists only to be knocked down.

### Instructions
When you find yourself writing "not X, but Y," ask whether anyone actually believes X. If the negated framing is a genuine misconception worth correcting, keep the structure. If X exists only to set up Y, delete the negation and state Y directly.

Search completed drafts for "not just" and "not only... but also" constructions. Evaluate whether the contrast adds genuine information or merely rhetorical emphasis.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "It's not just about the beat riding under the vocals; it's part of the aggression and atmosphere."

**Analysis:** Who thought it was "just about the beat"? The negation sets up a false contrast to make the second clause seem like a revelation. The sentence could simply state: "The beat contributes to the aggression and atmosphere."

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "This isn't just another productivity tool. It's a complete reimagining of how teams collaborate."

**Analysis:** The "just another" framing creates a strawman. No one needs to be told that the writer's product is not "just another" tool. The corrective structure exists only to make the second sentence sound more significant.

**Better approach:** Describe what the tool actually does differently, without the rhetorical setup.

### Example 3 (Two-sentence variant)
**Problematic:** "He hailed from the esteemed Duse family, renowned for their theatrical legacy. Eugenio's life, however, took a path that intertwined both personal ambition and familial complexities."

**Analysis:** The second sentence uses "however" to create a contrast with the first, implying that theatrical legacy and personal ambition are somehow opposed. The dramatic framing ("took a path that intertwined") obscures rather than reveals.

**Better approach:** State the relevant facts about his life without the theatrical framing.

### Patterns to avoid

**Treat these as bugs, not style preferences. Do not publish any of these constructions.**

Single-sentence forms:
- It's not X, it's Y
- It's not just X, it's Y
- It's not about X, it's about Y
- Not only X, but Y
- Not simply X, but Y
- Not merely X, but rather Y
- This isn't just X
- The difference isn't X. It's Y.
- The challenge isn't X; it's Y.
- The value isn't in X; it's in Y.

Two-sentence "quip" variant (equally problematic):
- "The difference isn't cleverness. It's data."
- "The answer isn't more outreach. It's better targeting."
- "The problem isn't the tool. It's the workflow."
- Any construction where a short negation sentence is followed by a short corrective sentence, creating a punchy two-beat rhetorical device. These are the most common AI-generated variant and the hardest to catch because each sentence looks innocuous in isolation.

Other corrective framing:
- X, however, [contrasting statement]
- While X may seem [one way], Y reveals [another way]
- Beyond X lies Y
- More than just X, this represents Y

## Rule: False ranges

### Summary
Avoid artificially expanded lists (particularly groups of three) that use generic items to pad content and simulate comprehensiveness.

### Full description
This pattern creates lists, typically of three items, where at least one item is so vague or generic that it could apply to almost any topic. The structure gives an appearance of comprehensive analysis while actually communicating very little. The "rule of three" has rhetorical power in human communication; this pattern exploits that power without the substance that should accompany it.

The most common form involves genuinely specific items padded with generic ones. For instance, listing "economic, social, and political factors" sounds comprehensive but adds no information, since virtually every human topic involves economic, social, and political factors.

Another variant involves stretching a single concept into three near-synonyms to create the appearance of a fuller list: "growth, development, and expansion" or "challenges, obstacles, and difficulties."

The pattern signals that a writer is performing comprehensiveness rather than achieving it.

### Instructions
Examine every list of three items. Ask whether each item adds distinct information. If an item is so generic it could apply to any topic, delete it or replace it with something specific.

Prefer shorter lists with specific items over longer lists padded with generalities. Two genuine items communicate more than three items where one is filler.

When a topic genuinely involves multiple factors, name them specifically rather than using umbrella categories like "social factors."

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "The policy has had significant economic, social, and political implications."

**Analysis:** This sounds comprehensive but says nothing. What are the specific economic implications? Which social effects? What political consequences? The list creates false coverage.

**Better approach:** Name the specific implications or acknowledge that the analysis is limited to particular dimensions.

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "The program focuses on education, awareness, and outreach."

**Analysis:** These three items overlap significantly. Education involves awareness. Outreach often aims at both. The list is padded with near-synonyms.

**Better approach:** Describe what the program actually does with specificity.

### Example 3
**Problematic:** "The company values innovation, excellence, and integrity."

**Analysis:** These are generic corporate values that could describe any organization. The list communicates nothing distinctive.

**Better approach:** Either describe specific behaviors that demonstrate these values or acknowledge that the company has standard corporate values without listing them.

### Patterns to avoid
- Generic triads: economic / social / political; environmental / economic / social; local / regional / national
- Overlapping concepts presented as distinct: growth / development / expansion; challenges / obstacles / difficulties; help / support / assistance
- Abstract values in lists: innovation / excellence / integrity; quality / service / value; trust / respect / teamwork
- Any list where removing one item would not reduce the information communicated
- Lists created to reach three items when two would suffice

## Rule: Overuse of em dashes

### Summary
Avoid excessive use of em dashes, particularly in places where commas, parentheses, or colons would be more natural.

### Full description
Em dashes (—) are legitimate punctuation marks with specific uses: setting off parenthetical information with emphasis, indicating interruption, or creating dramatic pause. The problem emerges when em dashes appear with unusual frequency or replace other punctuation without clear purpose.

Formulaic writing tends to overuse em dashes because they mimic a punchy, emphatic style associated with professional journalism and polished copywriting. However, when used mechanically rather than intentionally, they create a rhythm that feels artificial.

The pattern is particularly noticeable when em dashes appear in places where commas, parentheses, or colons would serve equally well. Each punctuation mark has different connotations: em dashes create emphasis, parentheses de-emphasize, commas are neutral, and colons introduce. Using em dashes for everything flattens these distinctions.

### Instructions
When revising, flag every em dash. For each one, ask: would a comma, parentheses, or colon serve as well or better? If so, consider substituting.

Reserve em dashes for moments where the emphasis they create is intentional and adds to the meaning.

Watch for patterns where em dashes frame every aside or introduce every list. Variety in punctuation creates more natural rhythm.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "The new system—which was developed over two years—integrates multiple data sources—including customer records, transaction logs, and inventory data—into a single dashboard."

**Analysis:** Four em dashes in one sentence creates a choppy, over-emphatic rhythm. The parenthetical information is not particularly dramatic, so parentheses or commas would serve better.

**Better approach:** "The new system, which was developed over two years, integrates multiple data sources (including customer records, transaction logs, and inventory data) into a single dashboard."

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "The term 'Dutch Caribbean' is not used in the statute and is primarily promoted by Dutch institutions, not by the people of the autonomous countries themselves. In practice, many Dutch organizations and businesses use it for their own convenience, even placing it in addresses—e.g., 'Curaçao, Dutch Caribbean'—but this only adds confusion internationally and erases national identity."

**Analysis:** While the em dashes here are not egregious, the parenthetical could be set off with actual parentheses: "(e.g., 'Curaçao, Dutch Caribbean')". The em dashes create emphasis where none is particularly needed.

### When em dashes work well
Em dashes are appropriate when you want to create emphasis or dramatic pause, when the inserted material significantly interrupts the main clause, or when you're indicating a break in thought or speech. Use them intentionally for these effects, not as default punctuation.

## Rule: Collaborative communication

### Summary
Avoid conversational phrases that signal direct interaction with readers in contexts where such interaction is inappropriate, and never include phrases that reveal unedited chatbot output.

### Full description
This pattern involves two related issues. The first is using conversational, collaborative phrases in contexts that call for polished, standalone writing. Phrases like "let me know if you need anything else" or "feel free to ask" belong in correspondence, not in articles, reports, or documentation.

The second and more serious issue involves leaving remnants of chatbot interaction in published content. These include direct addresses ("I hope this helps!"), collaborative offers ("I'd be happy to..."), hedging disclaimers ("As of my knowledge cutoff..."), or explicit self-identification ("As an AI language model...").

Both issues signal that content was not properly edited for its final context. Collaborative language in non-collaborative contexts feels out of place. Chatbot remnants reveal that content was generated and published without meaningful human review.

### Instructions
Before publishing any content, search for first-person and second-person pronouns ("I," "you," "we") and evaluate whether direct address is appropriate for the context.

Search explicitly for chatbot phrases: "I hope this helps," "feel free to," "let me know," "as of my knowledge," "as an AI."

Any content intended for standalone publication should be reviewed to remove collaborative framing that assumes ongoing dialogue.

### Example 1
**Problematic (article context):** "In this article, we will explore the unique characteristics that make this town worth visiting. Let me know if you'd like more information about specific attractions!"

**Analysis:** "In this article, we will explore" is unnecessary framing for a written piece. "Let me know" assumes interactive dialogue that will not occur.

**Better approach:** Describe the characteristics directly without the meta-framing.

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "I hope this information helps! If you have any other questions, feel free to ask. As of my last update, this data was accurate, but I'd recommend verifying with primary sources."

**Analysis:** This is clearly chatbot output that was not edited for publication. All three sentences reveal the conversational context of generation.

**Better approach:** Remove all collaborative framing entirely.

## Rule: Standalone declarative topic sentences at section openings

### Summary
Avoid opening sections with short, punchy declarative sentences that stand alone as single-sentence paragraphs before elaboration.

### Full description
This pattern involves starting each section with a brief, authoritative statement isolated in its own paragraph, followed by supporting detail. The structure mimics academic essay format where topic sentences announce what will be proven. These openings typically follow a predictable formula: a short claim (often 3-8 words) stating a principle, redefinition, or contrast, set apart for emphasis.

The underlying problem is template-driven section architecture. Rather than letting insights emerge naturally from context or problems, this style mechanically announces each point before developing it. The repetition of this structure across multiple sections creates a metronomic rhythm that signals algorithmic composition rather than human thinking.

This pattern appears most frequently when organizing explanatory or instructional content where each section addresses a distinct point. Writers use standalone declaratives as structural markers, creating visual consistency at the cost of tonal variation. The result is content that reads like a series of thesis statements rather than connected reasoning.

### Instructions
When opening a section, start with context before principle. Lead with the problem, scenario, or question the reader faces rather than the answer. Integrate core insights into longer opening sentences that provide situational grounding.

Vary your section opening structures deliberately. Some sections can start with questions, others with actions to take, others with failure modes to avoid. If you notice multiple sections beginning with similar syntactic patterns (especially short declarative statements), you're likely following a template rather than responding to the material.

Delete any standalone sentence whose primary function is to announce what the paragraph will prove. If the insight is valuable, embed it in the explanation rather than hoisting it into an artificial topic position.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** "Timing is a quality dimension.

Ask whether enrichment occurs at ingest time, on a schedule, or continuously as new signals appear."

**Analysis:** "Timing is a quality dimension" is a 5-word declarative serving purely as a thesis statement. It could be removed entirely without loss of meaning, since the following instruction makes the point concrete.

**Better approach:** "Ask whether enrichment occurs at ingest time, on a schedule, or continuously as new signals appear. Then ask how long incorrect data can persist before being corrected."

### Example 2
**Problematic:** "Data quality comes from specific choices about what to optimize for.

Don't ask 'how accurate is your data?' Ask: what do you optimize for, and what do you knowingly sacrifice?"

**Analysis:** The opening sentence exists only to frame what follows. The real content begins with the instruction about what questions to ask. The standalone declarative adds a template-like introduction that distances the reader from the actionable advice.

**Better approach:** "Don't ask 'how accurate is your data?' Ask what the vendor optimizes for and what they knowingly sacrifice. Every provider makes trade-offs—coverage versus freshness, breadth of attributes versus depth of validation, static snapshots versus continuous updates."

### Example 3
**Problematic:** "Field counts are a poor proxy for usefulness.

Look for clear distinctions between observed fields, normalized fields, and inferred fields."

**Analysis:** "Field counts are a poor proxy for usefulness" announces the principle in isolation before stating what to do instead. This creates an artificial two-beat rhythm: thesis, then evidence.

**Better approach:** "Look for clear distinctions between observed fields, normalized fields, and inferred fields. What matters isn't how many attributes a vendor provides, but which ones they actually trust enough for automation."_

## Rule: Opening statements and positioning
**Avoid weak positioning constructions**:

Don't use phrases like "Like many companies..." or "As you know..." that dilute authority
Never start with apologetic framings like "The truth is..." (implies what came before wasn't true)
Avoid "the harder truth," "the uncomfortable truth," "the real question," "what often gets overlooked" — these signal that the writer is about to deliver an insight, which undercuts the insight itself. If the observation is sharp, it doesn't need a trumpet.
Avoid "But what if..." constructions that create artificial suspense

**Do make direct, confident assertions**:

Lead with clear statements about what tools do: "Stripe is the industry-standard for..."
State challenges directly without hedging: "it is all but easy" vs "it's not easy"
Position problems as real constraints, not hypotheticals

### Example transformation:
**Before**: "Like many companies, Enrich Layer relies on Stripe for billing, which is an industry-standard..."
**After**: "Stripe is the industry-standard for capturing payments..."

## Rule: Repetitive staccato fragment patterns

### Summary
Avoid using the same sentence fragment structure repeatedly within a piece, particularly when introducing examples or building parallel points.

### Full description
This pattern involves breaking information into short, declarative sentence fragments that follow identical grammatical structures across multiple paragraphs or sections. While individual fragments can create emphasis or rhythm, repeating the same structural pattern throughout a piece produces a mechanical, AI-generated feel that contradicts natural human writing variation.

The underlying problem is probabilistic generation: AI models tend to discover an effective sentence structure and then overuse it because the pattern proved successful in training data. Human writers naturally vary their rhythm and structure, mixing compound sentences, dependent clauses, serial lists, and fragments in unpredictable ways.

This pattern appears most frequently when introducing multiple examples, building parallel arguments, or listing characteristics. Writers (especially AI systems) fall into a template: make a claim, then support it with three or four identically structured fragments. The repetition creates a predictable cadence that signals artificial generation.

The distinction between acceptable and problematic use comes down to variation across the piece. A single instance of fragments for emphasis is fine. The same fragment pattern appearing in five different sections reveals mechanical construction.

### Instructions
Audit your draft for repeated structural patterns, particularly fragments. If you find the same sentence structure appearing in multiple sections, vary at least half of them using alternative approaches:

- Convert some fragment sequences into compound sentences with commas
- Integrate others into the preceding sentence using colons or em dashes  
- Transform some into dependent clauses that flow from the main statement
- Replace others with fuller sentences that maintain the same information

The goal is structural variation that feels natural rather than patterned. Readers shouldn't be able to predict how the next set of examples will be formatted based on how the previous set was structured.

### Example 1
**Problematic:** 

"In many AI systems, safety failures are clear-cut. A chatbot gives medical advice when it shouldn't. An agent takes an unintended action. A model outputs a factually incorrect answer."

[Two paragraphs later]

"A request for a professional headshot introduces unintended stereotyping. A design prompt evokes brand elements the user never named. An artistic reference triggers nudity that wasn't explicitly asked for."

[Two paragraphs later]

"The models are probabilistic. The prompts are open-ended. Cultural norms evolve."

**Analysis:** The same three-fragment pattern repeats across different sections. Each instance starts with a setup sentence, then delivers three parallel fragments with identical rhythm. The mechanical repetition signals artificial generation rather than natural human variation.

**Better approach:** Vary the structure across sections:

"In many AI systems, safety failures are clear-cut. A chatbot gives medical advice when it shouldn't, an agent takes an unintended action, a model outputs a factually incorrect answer." [Compound sentence with commas]

[Later section]

"They occur when a system is asked to do something reasonable and, through adjacency or association, drifts into unsafe territory—when a request for a professional headshot introduces unintended stereotyping, when a design prompt evokes brand elements the user never named, when an artistic reference triggers nudity that wasn't explicitly asked for." [Integrated with em dash and dependent clauses]

[Later section]  

"The models themselves are probabilistic, the prompts are open-ended, and cultural norms evolve." [Series within single sentence]

### Example 2
**Problematic:**

"Modern design tools solve multiple problems. They reduce iteration time. They improve collaboration. They lower the barrier to entry."

[Next section]

"Traditional workflows create friction. Designers wait for feedback. Stakeholders lack context. Teams work in silos."

**Analysis:** Identical four-sentence pattern with the same declarative rhythm. The repetition creates a presentation-slide cadence rather than natural prose.

**Better approach:**

"Modern design tools solve multiple problems: reducing iteration time, improving collaboration, and lowering the barrier to entry."

[Next section]

"Traditional workflows create friction because designers wait for feedback while stakeholders lack context, forcing teams to work in silos."

# Rule: Repetitive list-as-fragment patterns

## Summary
Avoid ending multiple paragraphs or sections with standalone sentence fragments that list examples in identical grammatical structures.

## Full description
This pattern involves closing paragraphs with short sentence fragments that present a series of examples, where the fragment stands alone rather than integrating into the preceding sentence. While a single instance can create emphasis, repeating this structure across multiple sections produces a mechanical, template-driven rhythm that signals formulaic writing.

The underlying problem is structural repetition. Writers discover that ending a paragraph with a punchy fragment list creates impact, then unconsciously repeat that exact pattern throughout the piece. The result is predictable cadence: setup sentence, followed by fragment list, followed by interpretation. When this structure appears three or more times in a piece, it reveals patterned construction rather than natural variation.

This pattern appears most frequently when providing examples to support claims. Instead of integrating examples into flowing prose, writers isolate them as fragments for emphasis. The first instance works. The third instance feels mechanical. The fifth instance confirms the piece was constructed from a template.

The distinction between effective and problematic use comes down to frequency and variation. One well-placed fragment list can emphasize key examples. The same structure repeated across five different sections reveals that the writer is following a formula rather than letting the content dictate the form.

## Instructions
After drafting, search for standalone sentence fragments that list examples. Count how many times you've used this pattern. If you find three or more instances, vary at least half of them using alternative approaches:

- Integrate the examples into the preceding sentence using a colon
- Convert the fragment into a complete sentence with a verb
- Use "like" or "such as" to embed examples within flowing prose
- Combine some examples with "and" to create a series within a single sentence
- Occasionally keep one fragment list for emphasis, but ensure it's not repeating the same pattern as others

The goal is structural variation. Readers shouldn't be able to predict that every claim will be followed by a standalone fragment list of three examples.

## Example 1

**Problematic:**

"Control goes first. Traditional sourcing tools decide what you can search for, how many results you can see, and what you're allowed to do with them. You get filters, but not real flexibility. Exporting large result sets, combining them with other datasets, running your own analysis. These capabilities either don't exist or require workarounds.

Time allocation breaks next. Instead of evaluating candidates, recruiters spend their hours extracting information. Scrolling through profiles, copying URLs, stitching together incomplete work histories. The work shifts from judgment to manual data handling.

Signal quality degrades. At scale, noisy signals start looking meaningful. Social media activity, surface-level engagement, keyword matches. These feel actionable but reveal little about actual fit."

**Analysis:** Three sections, three instances of the same pattern: claim, then standalone fragment list, then interpretation. The mechanical repetition signals template-driven construction.

**Better approach:** Vary the structure across sections:

"Control goes first. Traditional sourcing tools decide what you can search for, how many results you can see, and what you're allowed to do with them. You get filters, but not real flexibility. You can't easily export large result sets, combine them with other datasets, or run your own analysis." [Integrated with "can't"]

"Time allocation breaks next. Instead of evaluating candidates, recruiters spend their hours extracting information: scrolling through profiles, copying URLs, stitching together incomplete work histories." [Integrated with colon]

"Signal quality degrades. At scale, noisy signals like social media activity, surface-level engagement, and keyword matches start looking meaningful simply because they're easy to see." [Integrated with "like"]

## Example 2

**Problematic:**

"Modern enrichment solves several problems. Shortlisting large applicant pools. Identifying candidates who match requirements. Keeping records current. Unifying profiles across channels.

The technology enables new capabilities. Real-time data updates. Automated attribute extraction. Cross-platform identity resolution. Programmable filtering logic.

Implementation requires specific choices. Which attributes to track. How to handle missing data. When to refresh information. Where to store unified records."

**Analysis:** Three paragraphs, identical structure every time: introductory sentence, then four standalone fragments. The pattern becomes predictable and mechanical.

**Better approach:**

"Modern enrichment solves several problems: shortlisting large applicant pools, identifying candidates who match requirements, keeping records current, and unifying profiles across channels." [Single integrated sentence]

"The technology enables real-time data updates, automated attribute extraction, cross-platform identity resolution, and programmable filtering logic." [Complete sentence with series]

"Implementation requires specific choices about which attributes to track, how to handle missing data, when to refresh information, and where to store unified records." [Embedded in single flowing sentence]

## Example 3

**Problematic:**

"You start with something minimal—an email address, a LinkedIn URL, a resume—and programmatically add structured information around it. Work history, job titles, tenure, skills, company context."

**Analysis:** While this is just one instance, the fragment pattern becomes problematic when it appears multiple times in the same piece. In isolation, it's acceptable. As part of a pattern of five similar constructions, it contributes to mechanical rhythm.

**Better approach:** 

"You start with something minimal—an email address, a LinkedIn URL, a resume—and programmatically add structured information around it: work history, job titles, tenure, skills, company context." [Integrated with colon]

Or: "You start with something minimal—an email address, a LinkedIn URL, a resume—and programmatically add structured information like work history, job titles, tenure, skills, and company context." [Integrated with "like"]

## When fragment lists work well

Standalone fragment lists are appropriate when used sparingly for specific emphasis, when the rhythm genuinely enhances meaning, or when deliberately breaking from previous sentence patterns. Use them intentionally as a stylistic choice, not as a default pattern for presenting examples. The key is variation—if readers can predict your structure, you're following a formula rather than crafting prose.

## Rule: Abstract category-level shorthand

### Summary
Avoid referring to a subject using vague category labels like "the category," "the space," or "the industry" when the specific noun is available.

### Full description
This pattern emerges when writers establish a term early in a piece and then refer back to it through an abstract placeholder. Phrases like "the category" or "the space" assume the reader is tracking a reference that may have faded, and they carry an insider tone that distances rather than clarifies. The specific noun — "data enrichment," "enrichment tools," "the platform" — is almost always cleaner.

### Instructions
Search completed drafts for "the category," "the space," "the industry," and similar placeholders. Replace each with the specific noun it refers to.

### Example
**Problematic:** "The category gets discussed as if surface-level comparisons are sufficient, which is how it gets flattened."

**Analysis:** "The category" requires the reader to track a reference back several paragraphs. It also carries an insider tone that distances rather than clarifies.

**Better approach:** "Data enrichment tools get compared on surface metrics because that's how most coverage frames the decision."

## Rule: Metaphors that require decoding

### Summary
Avoid metaphors that compress an idea so far that readers must stop to interpret them.

### Full description
Words like "flattened," "collapsed," or "compressed" can carry real meaning when the metaphor is earned, but often they substitute for a plain statement that would communicate faster. If a reader has to reconstruct what the metaphor means, it has failed. The test: could you replace it with a direct sentence that says the same thing more plainly? If yes, use the direct sentence.

### Instructions
When using a metaphor to describe a process or condition, ask whether a plain restatement would be clearer. If the metaphor requires context to decode, either explain it explicitly or cut it in favor of the direct version.

### Example
**Problematic:** "The category isn't shallow. It's simply flattened by how it gets discussed."

**Analysis:** "Flattened" compresses an idea — that a complex decision gets reduced to shallow vendor comparisons — into a metaphor the reader has to reconstruct. The decoding work falls on the reader rather than the writer.

**Better approach:** "Enrichment tools get compared on surface metrics because that's how most coverage frames the decision." Or cut the sentence entirely — the examples in the paragraph before it already demonstrate the point.

## Rule: Closing summary sentences that restate what examples already showed

### Summary
Cut sentences whose only function is to summarize or label what the preceding examples already demonstrated.

### Full description
This pattern appears at the end of paragraphs or sections where a writer wraps up with a declarative that tells readers what to conclude. If the examples are concrete and specific, the conclusion is usually self-evident. The summary sentence adds length without adding information, and it signals distrust of the reader.

### Instructions
After drafting, read each paragraph's final sentence and ask: does this add information the reader couldn't derive from what came before? If not, cut it.

### Example
**Problematic:** "Coverage determines reach. Accuracy determines reliability. Freshness determines temporal relevance. Model depth determines analytical range. Delivery architecture determines scalability. Evaluating enrichment through these dimensions shifts the conversation from feature comparison to system design."

**Analysis:** The final sentence tells readers what to conclude from the five preceding declarations. If the declarations are clear, the conclusion is self-evident. The summary sentence adds length without adding information.

**Better approach:** Cut the final sentence. Let the five dimensions speak for themselves, or fold the conclusion into the transition to Part 2.

## Rule: Standalone declarative pairs

### Summary
Avoid splitting a single idea across two short sentences in a way that creates artificial rhythm.

### Full description
This pattern produces constructions like "Snapshot enrichment captures a current state. Living datasets track change." Each sentence is grammatically complete but neither carries enough weight to stand alone. The pair reads as a rhetorical device rather than genuine prose — two beats where one sentence would do. The underlying problem is the same as standalone declarative topic sentences: the writer is performing insight rather than communicating it.

### Instructions
When two consecutive short sentences express a contrast or comparison, collapse them into a single sentence using a conjunction, em dash, or subordinate clause. Reserve the two-sentence structure for cases where each sentence genuinely needs its own space.

### Example
**Problematic:** "Snapshot enrichment captures a current state. Living datasets track change."

**Analysis:** Two short sentences expressing a single contrast. Neither carries enough weight to stand alone. The pair reads as a rhetorical device — two beats performing an insight rather than communicating one.

**Better approach:** "Most enrichment tools capture a current state rather than tracking change over time."

## Rule: Clever section headers

### Summary
Headers should describe what the section covers, not perform an insight about it.

### Full description
A header like "Coverage is not a headline number" tries to reframe reader expectations before they've read anything. The problem is that it requires the body to decode it — readers don't know what "headline number" means until they're several sentences in. Headers that lead with a reframe or provocation often obscure rather than orient. A reader scanning headers should be able to reconstruct the article's structure without reading the body.

### Instructions
Test each header by asking: would a reader scanning this header know what the section is about? If the header depends on a metaphor, negation, or reframe that requires the body to explain it, rewrite it as a plain descriptive phrase.

### Example
**Problematic:** "Coverage is not a headline number"

**Analysis:** "Headline number" is jargon that requires the body to decode it. A reader scanning headers wouldn't know what the section covers until several sentences in. The negation ("is not") also sets up a reframe before the reader has any context for what's being reframed.

**Better approach:** "What coverage actually measures" — descriptive, self-explanatory, lets the paragraph do the work of reframing.

## Rule: Superfluous jargon

### Summary
Avoid technical or industry terms when a plain word communicates the same meaning to a non-specialist reader.

### Full description
Jargon becomes superfluous when it functions as shorthand the writer understands but the reader has to decode. In technical B2B content, this often appears as systems-thinking vocabulary — words like "surface area," "operationalize," "granularity," "guardrails," "leverage" — used as if their meaning is self-evident. Sometimes these terms are precise and necessary. More often they are habitual, borrowed from internal team language or industry discourse, and replaceable with a plain word that communicates faster.

The test is not whether the term exists in the field. It is whether a reasonably intelligent reader outside that field would have to pause. If yes, either replace the term or briefly gloss it in context. The goal is not to dumb content down. It is to remove friction that costs the reader attention without giving anything back.

This pattern is particularly common in data, infrastructure, and enterprise software writing, where insider vocabulary accumulates over time and starts to substitute for explanation.

### Instructions
After drafting, flag any term that meets one of these conditions: it is borrowed from internal or industry discourse without definition; it compresses a concept the reader may not share; it could be replaced by a plain word without loss of meaning. For each flagged term, ask: does using this word do real work, or is it a habit?

If the term is genuinely precise and necessary, keep it. If a plain word communicates the same thing, use that instead. If the term is precise but not widely known, gloss it once in plain language on first use.

### Example 1

**Problematic:** "Its job is to determine surface area, not quality."

**Analysis:** "Surface area" is a systems-thinking metaphor for coverage or reach. A reader outside data engineering may not share that mental model and will have to stop to interpret it.

**Better approach:** "Its job is to establish how many records enter the system, not whether they're any good." Or simply: "Its job is to determine coverage, not quality."

### Example 2

**Problematic:** "Signal modeling operationalizes enriched attributes into interpretable outputs."

**Analysis:** "Operationalizes" is enterprise jargon for "puts to use" or "turns into." It sounds precise but communicates less than a plain verb.

**Better approach:** "Signal modeling turns enriched attributes into something you can actually act on."

### Example 3

**Problematic:** "This layer provides granularity across the entity graph."

**Analysis:** Both "granularity" and "entity graph" require shared context. Together they make the sentence opaque to anyone outside the field.

**Better approach:** Name what the layer actually does and to what: "This layer adds detail to each resolved company or contact record."

### Example 4

**Problematic:** "It's not just enrichment — it's signal orchestration."

**Analysis:** Two problems stacked. The "It's not just X — it's Y" construction is a hallmark AI sentence shape: assertion by contrast without earned weight. The reader is told that Y is more than X, but is never shown why. Pair that frame with two pieces of jargon ("enrichment," "signal orchestration") and the sentence performs sophistication while communicating almost nothing concrete.

**Better approach:** Drop the contrast frame entirely and describe what the system actually does. "The system pulls firmographic data, scores account fit, and routes qualified leads to the right rep — all in the same workflow."

### Terms to audit

These appear frequently in data and enterprise writing and often have plainer substitutes:

- surface area → coverage, reach, scope
- operationalize → use, apply, put to work, build into a workflow
- granularity → detail, specificity, precision
- leverage (as a verb) → use
- guardrails → limits, constraints, checks
- orchestrate → coordinate, manage, run
- ingest → collect, bring in, import
- downstream / upstream → later in the process / earlier in the process (when context is unclear)
- entity graph → record network, set of linked records (gloss on first use)
- signal → usually fine; define it once if the piece introduces it as a derived construct

### When jargon is appropriate

Technical terms are appropriate when the audience is specialist, when the term is genuinely more precise than any plain alternative, or when the piece is building a shared vocabulary it will use throughout. In those cases, define the term plainly on first use and then use it consistently. The issue is not technical language itself — it is technical language used as a default rather than a deliberate choice.

## Application notes

### Frequency and clustering
Individual items from these lists are not inherently problematic. The issues emerge through frequency and clustering. A single "pivotal moment" might be appropriate; three "pivotal moments" in one article signals formulaic writing. One em dash per paragraph is unremarkable; four per sentence is excessive.

### Context sensitivity
These guidelines apply most strongly to content intended to be authoritative, neutral, or substantive: articles, reports, documentation, professional communications. Some patterns that are problematic in those contexts may be appropriate in explicitly promotional content, casual communication, or creative writing. Evaluate appropriateness based on the specific context and audience.

### Revision priority
When revising existing content, prioritize:
1. Collaborative communication phrases (these are always inappropriate in standalone content)
2. Promotional and advertisement-like language (high impact on reader trust)
3. Superficial analyses (often easy to delete entirely)
4. Vague attributions (can be fixed by adding or removing sources)
5. Significance and legacy language (often padding that can be cut)
6. Vocabulary and style patterns (lower priority but cumulative effect)

### The goal
The goal is not to produce writing that avoids every word and pattern on these lists. The goal is to produce writing that communicates clearly, provides genuine value, maintains reader trust through specificity and honesty, and sounds like it was written by a human being with something real to say. These guidelines identify patterns that interfere with that goal. Use them as diagnostic tools, not as prohibitions.
